# TTRPG Framework Document

## Core Concept
A one-shot tabletop RPG for ~6 kids (including preschool age) playing for approximately 2 hours. Classic fantasy setting with heroic adventure tone (bit silly, mostly earnest). Item and ability card-based system with simple success/fail mechanics.

---

## Thematic Foundation

### Literary & Spiritual Inspirations

#### C.S. Lewis (Narnia, Space Trilogy)
- Clear good vs evil, but evil is pitiable
- Nature responds to goodness - animals help, trees wake
- Hierarchy of being (some creatures wise and noble, some corrupted)
- Victory through sacrifice and right choices, not just strength
- The world has depth and magic woven through it

#### J.R.R. Tolkien
- **Eucatastrophe**: The sudden joyous turn, grace breaking through at the darkest moment
- **The Small and Humble Matter**: Kids don't need to be strongest, just truest. Courage in face of overwhelming odds
- **The Long Defeat**: Fighting for good because it's right, not because victory is guaranteed
- **Mercy and Pity**: Kindness to enemies ripples forward with unexpected consequences
- **Creation Sings**: The world itself has dignity, responds to goodness through beauty
- **The Shadow Cannot Create**: Evil only mocks and corrupts; victory restores what was twisted

#### Pilgrim's Progress (John Bunyan)
- Journey as transformation - not just getting somewhere, but who you become
- Named locations with thematic meaning (Slough of Despond, Hill Difficulty, Valley of Humiliation)
- Companions and guides appear at right moments
- Some who seem good lead astray, some who seem rough are true friends
- Burden and release - achieving freedom/restoration, not just defeating enemies
- The adversary represents something (despair, pride, fear, cruelty)

#### Blaine Eldredge (The Paradise King)
- Richly descriptive, poetic, fairytale-like prose
- Wonder and beauty as primary experiences
- The mystical made accessible and inviting
- Nature as alive and responsive
- Sensory, immersive world-building
- GM narration style: not just "you enter a forest" but "ancient trees bend down to see you, their leaves whispering secrets in silver and gold"

#### John Eldredge (Ordinary Mystics, Wild at Heart)
- **The Heart's Desire for Adventure**: We're made for epic stories; adventure reveals who we really are
- **A Battle to Fight**: Everyone has a role; the fight matters and you matter in it
- **A Beauty to Rescue**: Something worth fighting FOR, not just AGAINST (restoring land, freeing oppressed, healing brokenness)
- **Ordinary Mystics**: Regular people having genuine spiritual experiences; the sacred breaking into everyday moments
- Not reserved for "special" people - ordinary kids awake to something greater

#### Fruits of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22-23)
Core positive themes woven throughout the story:
- **Love**: Caring for others, protecting the weak, sacrifice for friends
- **Joy**: Celebration, wonder, delight in adventure and discovery
- **Peace**: Resolving conflict wisely, bringing harmony
- **Patience**: Waiting for the right moment, not rushing, listening
- **Kindness**: Helping those in need, generosity, mercy to enemies
- **Goodness**: Doing right even when it's hard, integrity
- **Faithfulness**: Keeping promises, loyalty to friends, perseverance
- **Gentleness**: Strength used carefully, compassion, soft power
- **Self-Control**: Choosing wisely, resisting temptation, thinking before acting

## World & Lore

### The Deep Magic (Background Lore - Mostly Subtext)
- Ancient good power woven into the fabric of the world
- Source of heroes' gifts and abilities
- Flows naturally through those with hearts open to it
- Explains why heroes can do extraordinary things
- Shows through story, not explained directly
- Kids experience it as: warmth when being brave/kind, nature responding to them, rightness of good choices
- The world is enchanted, responsive, alive

### The Heroes
**[Name TBD - to be chosen during story development]**
- Options being considered: Bearers of the Way, Wayfarers, Keepers of the Light, The Called Ones, Walkers of the True Path
- Special because of what they are - gifted with purpose
- Their classes/abilities are bestowed gifts, not earned
- The world responds to them differently
- Ordinary kids, but awake to the Deep Magic
- Not chosen because they're perfect or powerful, just... open

### The Enemy/Boss
- Someone who rejected or twisted the Deep Magic
- Wanted power without connection, or refused to serve/wanted to be served
- Lost touch with the Deep Magic, now uses darker means
- Represents an obstacle to freedom/goodness (despair, pride, fear, cruelty)
- Pitiable, not just hateful
- Can only mock and corrupt, cannot create
- Victory restores what was corrupted

### Drachan the Accuser (The Enemy Behind the Enemy)
**Also known as: Drachan the Liar, The Accusing Dragon**

- The ultimate source of darkness and corruption in the world
- Not encountered in this campaign, but his influence is felt
- The boss may serve him, reference him, or be corrupted by his whispers
- Represents: Accusation, lies, despair, the voice that says "you're not good enough"
- In contrast to the Deep Magic which calls out the good in people, Drachan accuses and diminishes
- He cannot create, only corrupt and destroy
- His power is real but not ultimate - the Deep Magic is deeper and truer
- Boss might say things like: "Drachan promised me power" or "The Accuser told me I was right to take what I wanted"
- Sets up potential for future campaigns while adding weight to this one
- Gives theological depth: there's a reason evil exists and persists, but it's not the deepest truth

### NPC Thematic Framework (Galatians 5)

Every NPC embodies at least one fruit - either of the Spirit or of the flesh/darkness. This gives encounters thematic weight and makes each character memorable and meaningful.

#### Fruits of the Spirit (Galatians 5:22-23)
Love, Joy, Peace, Patience, Kindness, Goodness, Faithfulness, Gentleness, Self-Control

#### Fruits of the Flesh/Darkness (Galatians 5:19-21, adapted for kids)
- **Hatred** - Deep dislike, wanting to hurt others
- **Discord** - Causing fights, division, breaking friendships
- **Jealousy/Envy** - Wanting what others have, bitterness over others' success
- **Selfish Ambition** - Only caring about yourself, stepping on others to get ahead
- **Fits of Rage** - Uncontrolled anger, lashing out
- **Idolatry** - Worshiping false things, pursuing power above all
- **Deception/Manipulation** - Lying, twisting truth (kid-friendly version of witchcraft theme)

#### NPC Design Categories

**Friendly NPCs** - Embody 1-2 Fruits of the Spirit
- The wise guide who shows **Patience** and **Peace**
- The healer who demonstrates **Kindness** and **Gentleness**
- The loyal companion showing **Faithfulness** and **Love**
- The joyful innkeeper embodying **Joy** and **Goodness**

**Questionable Character** - Mixed, showing potential for both
- Shows a corrupted fruit (Jealousy, Selfish Ambition) but can be redeemed through kids' responses
- Or appears corrupted but actually has hidden Goodness/Faithfulness
- Tests the kids' discernment and mercy
- Their arc can demonstrate transformation

**The Boss** - Embodies 1-2 primary corrupted fruits
- Examples:
  - **Selfish Ambition** + **Discord**: Wants power, divides people, manipulates
  - **Rage** + **Hatred**: Consumed by anger at perceived wrongs
  - **Idolatry** + **Deception**: Serves Drachan, twists truth to gain followers
- Their defeat can show restoration - what they become when corruption is removed

**Minor Enemies/Obstacles** - Each represents a specific corruption
- Jealous goblin (Envy)
- Rage-filled troll (Fits of Rage)
- Deceptive sprite (Manipulation/Lies)
- Selfish bandit (Selfish Ambition)
- Discord sower (causes fights among the party or NPCs)

#### Implementation Notes
- Kids don't need to know the framework - they just experience compelling characters
- NPC behavior naturally demonstrates their fruit
- Victory over corrupted characters can show redemption/restoration when appropriate
- Friendly NPCs model the fruits kids should emulate
- Each encounter reinforces the thematic foundation without being preachy

## Game Structure

### Character Creation
- Each player picks a class [classes to be determined]
- Class determines starting item loadout (with some variety)
- Player chooses 2 abilities from class-specific deck
- Simple character sheet tracks items and abilities

### Core Mechanics

#### Items vs Abilities
**Items** = Physical tools (can be lost, traded, stolen)
- Examples: "Iron Sword", "Healing Potion", "Rope & Grappling Hook"
- Cards show use cases: "Good for: Fighting, Cutting, Blocking"

**Abilities** = Innate powers (can't be taken away)
- Examples: "Berserker Rage", "Arcane Blast", "Shadow Step"
- Cards show use cases: "Good for: Big damage, Breaking things, Scaring enemies"

#### Action Resolution
1. Player declares action ("I want to attack the goblin")
2. Player picks which item or ability to use ("Using my Iron Sword")
3. Rolls dice [specific mechanics to be determined: d20 + d6 system]
4. GM narrates outcome

#### Difficulty & Complexity
- **No HP tracking** - Success/fail binary outcomes for simplicity
- **GM manages hidden complexity**:
  - Enemy "health" tracked invisibly (3 hits, multiple heads, whatever fits the story)
  - Multi-step challenges for big moments (boss fights, tough obstacles)
  - Difficulty adjusted on the fly based on story needs
- **Target numbers** [to be determined during mechanics development]

### Progression System

#### Loot Drops
- **Random loot**: Stack of item cards players choose from when treasure found
- **Legendary items**: Reserved cards given directly by GM at key narrative moments
- Items carry forward through the session

#### Failure Consequences
Players experience setbacks without harsh penalties:
- **Narrative setbacks** (most common) - story continues with complications
- **Temporary item loss** - enemy takes item, recovered after battle
- **Catastrophic fails** - item lost permanently (falls into chasm, destroyed)

### Player Management
- Designed for 6 players
- Flexible attendance - kids can come and go as interest fluctuates
- Age-adaptive for younger players (preschool through older kids)
- GM adapts complexity on the fly for different ages

## Story Arc Structure (2 Hours)

### Act Breakdown
1. **Opening** (15 min)
   - Wake in safe place, meet friendly NPC with quest
   - Establish the call to adventure

2. **Journey** (30 min)
   - Travel with small encounters
   - First loot drops
   - World-building through exploration

3. **Puzzle/Challenge** (20 min)
   - Non-combat obstacle requiring creative thinking
   - Meet questionable character (not clearly good/bad)

4. **Moral Choice** (15 min)
   - Decision point reflecting positive values
   - Consequences should ripple forward
   - Opportunity for mercy, kindness, patience, or self-control

5. **Boss Build-up** (10 min)
   - Approach final area with mounting tension
   - Legendary loot drop to prepare them

6. **Boss Fight** (20 min)
   - Climactic multi-step battle
   - Multiple phases or objectives
   - Requires teamwork and right choices, not just power

7. **Resolution** (10 min)
   - Victory and its aftermath
   - Beautiful narrative release (eucatastrophe moment)
   - Rest and restoration
   - "What happens next" epilogue

### Required Story Elements
- **Boss fight**: Multi-step, dramatic, thematically meaningful
- **Situational puzzles**: Can't just smash everything
- **Questionable character**: Tests judgment and mercy
- **Friendly NPCs**: Guides, companions who appear at right moments
- **Moral choice moment**: Should matter and reflect Fruits of the Spirit
- **Eucatastrophe ending**: Unexpected grace, beauty breaking through
- **Final rest**: Peace, restoration, freedom achieved

### Pilgrim's Progress Structure Principles
- Journey has purpose beyond destination
- Each location tests or teaches something
- Named places with thematic meaning
- Not just random encounters - each moment shapes the heroes
- The path itself transforms them
- Victory is about becoming, not just defeating

### Theme Integration (Subtle, Not Preachy)
Positive themes show through:
- **Kindness/Mercy in moral choice** - spare an enemy? Help someone who wronged you?
- **Patience with questionable character** - don't judge too quickly
- **Faithfulness in boss fight** - working together, not giving up
- **Gentleness/Self-Control in puzzles** - can't just use force
- **Joy throughout journey** - delight in friendship and adventure
- **Deep Magic grows stronger** - narrate warmth/rightness when kids make good choices

## GM Approach & Narration Style

### Narrative Voice
Inspired by Blaine Eldredge's poetic style:
- Richly descriptive and sensory
- Wonder and beauty as primary experiences
- Nature alive and responsive
- Immersive, fairytale-quality language
- Example: "Ancient trees bend down to see you, their leaves whispering secrets in silver and gold"

### Player Experience Goals
- Kids experience mystical moments naturally (feeling warmth, hearing whispers, seeing beauty)
- The world feels enchanted and responsive
- They're ordinary, but the Magic recognizes them
- Adventure reveals who they really are
- Every player matters in the fight
- Small and humble actions have weight

### GM Tools & Flexibility
- Read the table - adjust difficulty and pacing on the fly
- Younger kids get adaptive complexity
- Celebrate creativity and character choices
- Let consequences flow naturally from decisions
- Keep momentum - avoid getting bogged down
- Remember: you control hidden complexity, they experience story

## Design Principles

### Keep It Simple
- Binary success/fail outcomes
- No complex math or tracking
- GM handles hidden complexity
- Fast-moving, story-focused

### Keep It Meaningful
- Every choice matters narratively
- Themes woven naturally, not preached
- World responds to goodness
- Victory comes through right choices + courage

### Keep It Joyful
- Wonder and beauty throughout
- Silly moments within heroic frame
- Celebration of friendship and adventure
- Ends with rest and restoration

### Keep It Flexible
- Age-adaptive on the fly
- Players can come and go
- GM adjusts difficulty as needed
- Story over rules always
