Beren has been at this gate long enough that the days have stopped being separate. He does his job. He does not ask questions. He does not make trouble and does not look for it.

His neighbor lost everything to Dorogh's taxes.

He is not a bad man. He has simply run out of the kind of energy you need to be a good one. There is a difference.

## Scene 4 — East Market Gate

Beren is harder to crack than Tam but not impossible. He is not protecting Dorogh — he has no loyalty there. He is protecting his own continued survival, which requires not making decisions.

**Entry options:**
- **Heart or Wit 12+:** Tam cracks first; Beren follows when he sees Tam is not going to report it anyway. He will not be the one to make an enemy of travelers who have already gotten Tam's quiet approval.
- **Merchant's ID:** Beren barely looks at it. Waves them through.
- **Disguise:** Beren is harder to fool because he doesn't particularly care what the truth is. He just wants the shift to end.

## GM Notes

Beren is the picture of what prolonged, low-grade oppression does to ordinary people. He is not broken — he is worn smooth. Play him without judgment. He has his reasons.

If the liberation happens (scene 8), Beren is among the guards who simply stop fighting when Dorogh falls. He sits down on the gate steps and does not move for a long time. Not weeping. Not celebrating. Processing.

**Voice:** "State your business." Long pause. If they explain: "Fine." That's it. He is not interested in more than he needs.
