# Session 1 Debrief: Dawn of the Third Age
## Rjocht Campaign - Post-Session Review

---

## What Happened

The first session ran approximately **2 hours** and covered getting the party from outside the city to **inside the gates of Rjocht**. They never reached Jorik or the dungeon — the adventure will continue next session.

### Key Moments (in order)

1. **Opening info dump** — Dragged slightly. Too much lore delivered upfront before the action started.
2. **The Blade Gambit** — MuhDude (Blade-Bearer) bet a ruffian merchant that he couldn't lift the Blade of Righteous Judgment. Clever, character-driven, landed perfectly.
3. **Dancing for silver** — Party entertained the marketplace crowd to pass time while waiting for the guard change. Nat 20 Agility roll = spectacular, crowd-pleasing performance.
4. **The corrupt guard** — Drawn by the attention, a guard nearly took them to the dungeon. Wit roll to talk their way out came up short. They gave him the silver to avoid arrest. Correct consequence: they were supposed to keep a low profile and attracted maximum attention.
5. **Off the main path** — Party chose the woods over the main road (player-driven).
6. **Goblin camp** — Stumbled onto it. 3 goblins, one-phase combat. MuhDude landed a critical hit with the Blade and shattered one goblin. Right length for an intro battle.
7. **The Weasel Band** — GM-inserted encounter to address unexpected path choice. Party found a weasel tribe living in trees connected by rope bridges over a swamp. A player with CREATION ability noticed a struggling weasel, talked to him, and they threw a net to rescue him. Organic, thematic, beautiful.
8. **Sprint to the gate** — After market detour nearly cost them the guard change window. Nat 20 roll to sprint across in time. Cinematic.
9. **Inside Rjocht** — Session ended here.
---

## What Worked

### Mechanics
- **d20 + 2d6 pick higher** felt smooth and intuitive
- **Nat 20 moments** created genuine cinematic highs (dancing, sprint)
- **Ability cards** felt distinct and useful; players naturally reached for the right ones
- **3-goblin combat** took the right amount of time — not too long, satisfying
- **Impromptu quest item cards** (gamble card, dancing card) — written on the fly, worked fine
### Story & Player Engagement
- **Emergent gameplay** was strong: players invented the market entertainment, the blade gambit, the off-path route
- **One combat encounter** felt right — players were far more interested in social and problem-solving moments
- **The Blade Bearer role** created real roleplay opportunity; MuhDude's mouthy personality is perfect irony for a weapon of righteous judgment
- **Drachan's Teeth as MacGuffins** — players are already hooked on the idea of more artifacts out there
- **Consequence felt earned** — silver loss wasn't arbitrary punishment; it flowed from their choices (big flashy dancing = attention = corrupt guard)
### Thematic Elements
- Deep Magic remains **vague and mysterious** — room to build on it over time
- World feels responsive and alive (nature encounter with weasels)
- Fruits of the Spirit showing through play naturally without being stated
---

## What to Improve

### Opening
- **Too much front-loaded lore.** Future sessions should start in medias res or drip-feed world info through NPCs and discovery.
- Consider: wake up already on a task, learn about the world by encountering it
- The "you're special" feeling works better through NPC reactions than explanation
### Player Retention
- **Age sweet spot confirmed: 9-13, especially those who like fantasy**
- Preschool/very young kids engaged briefly then left — expected, not a problem
- One 12-year-old disengaged; 13-year-old fantasy fan was fully locked in
- Design primarily for the engaged older kids
### Consequence Design (minor refinement)
- Future "loss" moments could include **visible hope of recovery** or **narrative trade-off** to keep agency clear
- The silver worked fine here, but something like: "you see him pocket it" or "a nearby NPC saw that happen" gives threads to pull later

## Design Principles Confirmed

| Principle | Evidence |
|-----------|----------|
| One combat per session is enough | Players preferred social/puzzle encounters |
| Nat 20 = auto-succeed cinematically | Gate sprint, dancing both felt right as hero moments |
| Consequences from choices, not GM punishment | Silver loss was earned, not arbitrary |
| Players will go off-script — build tools not rails | Every major moment was player-initiated |
| Age 9-13 fantasy fans are the core audience | Younger kids drifted, older engaged stayed |
| Deep Magic works better shown than explained | Weasel rescue felt magical without mechanics |

## One Open Question

**Nat 20 ruling:** Currently treating as cinematic auto-success. Confirm this as house rule going forward:
- Nat 20 = succeed awesomely regardless of difficulty
- Nat 1 = fail spectacularly
- Everything else = degrees of success/failure against difficulty
This fits the heroic fantasy tone and age group well.
